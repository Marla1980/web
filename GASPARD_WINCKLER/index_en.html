Almost 20 years separate Portrait of a Man (1960) from Un Cabinet d’Amateur (1979), Georges Perec’s first and last novels respectively. Both works reflect the writer’s obsession with copying, reproduction, falsification and plagiarism. In the first, in a more visceral and dramatic way, Perec tells the tragic story of a tormented forger. In the second, as if twenty years of experience had given him greater calm and serenity, he explores the more playful side of these concepts. The work To Gaspard Winkler, Who Is Still Alive, consists of two parts: first, 150 copies of signatures of various artists and forgers, including Georges Perec’s and the author’s own. The second element that makes up the work is a text that reflects on these concepts that both obsessed Perec, a fictitious conversation between two characters elaborated exclusively with words taken from Portrait of a Man and Un Cabinet d’Amateur.
